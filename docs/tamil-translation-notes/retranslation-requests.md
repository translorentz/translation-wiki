## Request 2: Paragraph 6, Lines 96-97 -- Retranslation of the Evening/Morning Metaphor

### Priority: HIGH (currently incomprehensible)

### Tamil Source Text (Lines 96-98):
```
maalaiyannatu oor punmaiyum kaalai
kaNTooL maruLum vaNTu cuuZ nilaiyum
kanavu ena maruNTa en nence maappa
```

### Current English Translation (from Paragraph 6):
> "The wretchedness of evening, and the morning, the state of being surrounded by bees, astonishing those who saw it. My heart, bewildered as if in a dream"

### Problems:
1. The evening/morning contrast is a metaphor for transformation (misery to glory), not a time reference
2. The bees swarming = attracted to the bard's new fragrant garlands (showing his transformed state)
3. The onlookers' astonishment is at the bard's transformation, not at the bees

### Suggested Retranslation:
> The wretchedness that was like evening [transformed into] a morning state where onlookers marveled, as bees swarmed [my new garlands]. My heart, bewildered as if in a dream, was filled with joy --

### Notes:
- "maalaiyannatu oor punmaiyum" = "a certain wretchedness that was like evening" (metaphor: his former miserable state)
- "kaalaik kaNTooL maruLum" = "in the morning, those who saw [him] were astonished" (his transformed, adorned state)
- "vaNTu cuuZ nilaiyum" = "the state of being surrounded by bees" (bees attracted to his new fragrant garlands/flowers -- proof of transformation)

## Request 3: Paragraph 8, Opening Line -- Vocative Correction

### Priority: HIGH (grammatical misreading changes meaning)

### Tamil Source Text (Line 120-121):
```
celva cEERum em tol pati peyarntu ena
mellenak kiLanttanam aaka vallE
```

### Current English Translation:
> "As wealth departs and our ancient home is transformed, we spoke softly, quickly"

### Problems:
1. "celva" is a vocative: "O wealthy one" (addressing the king), not "wealth" as a noun subject
2. "cEERum" = "we shall go/depart" (first person plural future), not "departs" (third person)
3. "em tol pati peyarntu" = "returning to our ancient home" (the bard going home), not the home being transformed
4. The king is being addressed; the bard is announcing departure

### Suggested Retranslation:
> "O wealthy one, we shall depart, returning to our ancient home," we said softly, when quickly --

### Notes:
- "celva" = vocative of "celvan" (wealthy one/prosperous one) -- a standard form of address to a patron
- "cEERum" = "we will go" (first person plural)
- "tol pati peyarntu" = "having returned to [our] old home/settlement"
- "mellenak kiLanttanam" = "we spoke softly/gently" (the bard tentatively broaching departure)

## Request 4: Paragraph 15, Line 238 -- Narantham/Civet Error

### Priority: HIGH (factual error)

### Tamil Source Text (Line 238):
```
naRaiyum naranthamum akilum aaramum
```

### Current English Translation:
> "fragrant herbs, civet, agarwood, and sandalwood"

### Problems:
- "narantham" is citron/bitter orange (a well-attested Sangam aromatic fruit), NOT civet
- Civet is an animal-derived musk (from the civet cat) with no Tamil word resembling "narantham"
- The four items are ALL plant-derived aromatics carried by the Kaveri in flood

### Suggested Retranslation:
> "fragrant cyperus, citron, agarwood, and sandalwood"

### Notes:
- naRai = Cyperus rotundus (nut-grass, an aromatic root)
- narantham = citrus fruit, likely citron or bitter orange (Citrus medica or C. aurantium)
- akil = agarwood (Aquilaria agallocha)
- aaram = sandalwood (Santalum album)
- All four are plant aromatics washed from mountain slopes by the Kaveri's floodwaters

## Request 5: Paragraph 10, Lines 161-162 -- Garland Description

### Priority: MEDIUM (meaning potentially inverted)

### Tamil Source Text (Lines 161-162):
```
nuulin valavaa nuNangaril maalai
vaaL oLi muttamooTu paaTini aNiyak
```

### Current English Translation:
> "and a delicate, unstrung garland, with bright, lustrous pearls for the female bard to wear"

### Problems:
- "nuulin valavaa" -- "nuulin" means "of/with thread." "valavaa" is debated (could be "not skillful" = simple, OR "circular/wound")
- "nuNangaril" = "of fine, sparse [flowers]"
- Saying "unstrung" when "nuulin" (with thread) is present contradicts the Tamil
- The garland IS on a thread, but the thread is so fine and the flowers so sparse that it appears almost bare

### Suggested Retranslation:
> "and a garland of fine, sparse flowers on an almost-invisible thread, with bright pearls, for the paadini to wear"

### Notes:
- The key image is delicacy: flowers so few and thread so fine that the garland seems to float
- "vaaL oLi muttam" = pearls of bright/white luster (interspersed with the flowers)
- "paaTini aNiya" = for the paadini (female singer-companion) to wear/adorn herself with

## Request 6: Paragraph 11, Lines 166-167 -- Seven-Step Protocol

### Priority: MEDIUM (sequence confused)

### Tamil Source Text (Lines 166-168):
```
kaalinEEZaTip pincenRu koolinRu
taaRukaLaintu EERenRu EeRRi viiRupeRu
pEriyaaZ muRaiyuZik kaZippi
```

### Current English Translation:
> "he will follow seven steps behind you on foot, and remove the goad from the staff, and say, 'Mount!' and make you ascend, and with great splendor, after you have properly performed with your great yaazh"

### Problems:
1. The sequence should be clearer: (a) king walks seven steps on foot behind the bard's chariot; (b) removes obstructions/wipes the goad; (c) says "Mount!" and helps the bard up onto the chariot
2. "pEriyaaZ muRaiyuZik kaZippi" = after the bard has performed properly on the great yaazh (this comes AFTER mounting, as a condition/timeline, not simultaneously)

### Suggested Retranslation:
> "He will walk seven steps behind you on foot, clear the staff of obstructions, say 'Mount!' and set you upon [the chariot]. After you have performed in proper measure on the great yaazh, gaining great splendor --"

### Notes:
- "kaalin EEZ aTi pin cenRu" = on foot (kaalin), seven steps (EEZ aTi), following behind (pin cenRu)
- "koolin taaRu kaLaintu" = clearing/removing (kaLaintu) debris/obstructions (taaRu) from the staff/goad (koolin)
- "EER enRu EeRRi" = saying "mount/ascend" (EER enRu) and making [him] ascend (EeRRi)
- "viiRu peRu pEriyaaZ" = the great yaazh of earned splendor/fame

## Request 7: Paragraph 12, Line 187 -- VaNTal Specificity

### Priority: LOW (cultural specificity lost but sense correct)

### Tamil Source Text (Line 187):
```
iLaiyoor vaNTal ayaravum
```

### Current English Translation:
> "and youngsters play in the sand"

### Problems:
- "vaNTal" is not generic sand-play but a specific Sangam childhood game: building miniature houses/towns from sand
- This is a well-attested cultural practice referenced in multiple Sangam poems
- Losing this specificity diminishes the ethnographic value

### Suggested Retranslation:
> "and children build their sand-houses (vantal)"

### Notes:
- vaNTal = a specific game of building miniature houses, courtyards, and towns from sand
- Referenced in Kuruntokai, Akananuru, and other Sangam collections
- The diminutive domestic architecture mirrors the "real" settlements described around it

## Request 8: Paragraph 1, Line 23 -- Musical Technique "uRaZntum"

### Priority: LOW (uncertain but likely wrong)

### Tamil Source Text (Line 23):
```
vaariyum vaTittum unthiyum uRaZntum
```

### Current English Translation:
> "Scooping, straining, pushing, and clashing"

### Problems:
- "uRaZntum" (from uRaZtal) means "alternating" or "contending/vying" -- NOT "clashing"
- In musical context, this likely refers to alternating patterns or antiphonal exchange between strings
- "Clashing" implies dissonance or violence, which contradicts the "maruvu in paalai" (sweet paalai melody) described

### Suggested Retranslation:
> "scooping, straining, pushing, and alternating"

### Notes:
- vaariyum = scooping/sweeping (likely a glissando or arpeggio technique)
- vaTittum = filtering/straining (possibly damping or muting certain strings)
- unthiyum = pushing/projecting (possibly a sustaining or plucking technique)
- uRaZntum = alternating/exchanging (possibly antiphonal or call-and-response patterns)
- All four describe yaazh-playing techniques whose exact modern equivalents are debated
