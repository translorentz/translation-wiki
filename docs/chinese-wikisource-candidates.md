# Chinese Wikisource Text Candidates for Translation

**Compiled:** 2026-01-29
**Source:** zh.wikisource.org
**Criteria:** Important texts without complete English translations, not already in Deltoi

## Summary Statistics
- Total candidates: 50
- By category: Philosophy (10), Literature (15), Literary Criticism (5), Science/Medicine (5), Geography/Travel (4), History/Biji (11)

## Candidates

### Philosophy

#### 1. 論衡 (Lun Heng)
- **English:** Discourses Weighed in the Balance
- **Author/Era:** Wang Chong (王充), Eastern Han (27-97 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/論衡
- **Chapters/Length:** 85 subpages (85 chapters across 30 juan)
- **Why important:** The most systematic work of critical rationalism in ancient China. Wang Chong challenged superstition, Confucian orthodoxy, and popular belief with empirical reasoning. A foundational text for Chinese intellectual history.
- **English translation status:** Partial — Alfred Forke translated selections (1907, 1911). No complete modern English translation exists.
- **Notes:** Well-structured on Wikisource with individual chapter pages.

#### 2. 淮南子 (Huainanzi)
- **English:** The Masters of Huainan
- **Author/Era:** Liu An (劉安) and guests, Western Han (~139 BCE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/淮南子
- **Chapters/Length:** 23 subpages (21 chapters)
- **Why important:** A major Han dynasty philosophical compendium synthesizing Daoist, Confucian, and Legalist thought. Covers cosmology, governance, astronomy, geography, and mythology.
- **English translation status:** John S. Major et al. published a complete translation (2010, Columbia UP), but it is not open-access. No public domain translation exists.
- **Notes:** Good Wikisource content. The Major translation is expensive and academic-only.

#### 3. 管子 (Guanzi)
- **English:** The Book of Master Guan
- **Author/Era:** Attributed to Guan Zhong (管仲), compiled Warring States-Han
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/管子
- **Chapters/Length:** 88 subpages (76 extant chapters)
- **Why important:** A massive compendium covering statecraft, economics, law, agriculture, and philosophy. One of the most important texts for understanding ancient Chinese political economy.
- **English translation status:** W. Allyn Rickett translated the full text (Princeton, 1985/1998) but it is out of print and not open-access. No public domain translation.
- **Notes:** Large text, excellent coverage on Wikisource.

#### 4. 近思錄 (Jinsilu)
- **English:** Reflections on Things at Hand
- **Author/Era:** Zhu Xi (朱熹) and Lu Zuqian (呂祖謙), Song (1175)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/近思錄
- **Chapters/Length:** 18 subpages (14 juan)
- **Why important:** The essential Neo-Confucian anthology compiled by Zhu Xi himself, selecting key passages from the four Northern Song masters (Zhou Dunyi, Zhang Zai, Cheng Hao, Cheng Yi). The foundational reading guide for Neo-Confucian study.
- **English translation status:** Wing-tsit Chan translated it (Columbia, 1967) but not open-access. No public domain translation.
- **Notes:** Pairs naturally with Zhu Zi Yu Lei already in Deltoi.

#### 5. 明夷待訪錄 (Mingyi Daifang Lu)
- **English:** Waiting for the Dawn: A Plan for the Prince
- **Author/Era:** Huang Zongxi (黃宗羲), Ming-Qing transition (1663)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/明夷待訪錄
- **Chapters/Length:** 29 sections on single page (21 essays)
- **Why important:** The most radical political critique in late imperial China. Huang challenges the entire imperial system, arguing for constitutional limits on monarchy, local autonomy, and rule of law. Often called "China's Magna Carta."
- **English translation status:** Wm. Theodore de Bary translated it (Columbia, 1993) but not open-access. No public domain version.
- **Notes:** Short but extremely influential. Compact text ideal for translation.

#### 6. 焚書 (Fenshu)
- **English:** A Book to Burn
- **Author/Era:** Li Zhi (李贄), Ming (1590)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/焚書
- **Chapters/Length:** 7 subpages (6 juan)
- **Why important:** The most iconoclastic work of the late Ming. Li Zhi attacked Confucian hypocrisy, championed individual moral autonomy, and defended vernacular fiction and women's intellectual capacity. He was arrested for his views and died in prison.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Rivi Handler-Spitz et al. translated selections (2016).
- **Notes:** Moderate size, provocative content.

#### 7. 鹽鐵論 (Yantie Lun)
- **English:** Discourses on Salt and Iron
- **Author/Era:** Huan Kuan (桓寬), Western Han (~74 BCE debate, recorded ~49 BCE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/鹽鐵論
- **Chapters/Length:** 20 subpages (60 chapters in 10 juan)
- **Why important:** A record of the great debate between Confucian scholars and Legalist officials over government monopolies on salt and iron. The earliest extended economic debate in world history, covering trade, taxation, foreign policy, and morality.
- **English translation status:** Esson Gale translated it (1931, reprinted 1967) but the translation is dated and not widely available. No modern open-access version.
- **Notes:** Dialogue format makes it very readable.

#### 8. 潛夫論 (Qianfu Lun)
- **English:** Comments of a Recluse
- **Author/Era:** Wang Fu (王符), Eastern Han (c. 90-165 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/潛夫論
- **Chapters/Length:** 14 subpages (36 chapters in 10 juan)
- **Why important:** A penetrating social critique of Eastern Han dynasty corruption, nepotism, and moral decay. Wang Fu writes from outside the establishment, offering sharp observations on governance, education, and human nature.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Margaret Pearson translated selections.
- **Notes:** Good size for a translation project.

#### 9. 抱朴子 (Baopuzi)
- **English:** The Master Who Embraces Simplicity
- **Author/Era:** Ge Hong (葛洪), Eastern Jin (c. 317 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/抱朴子
- **Chapters/Length:** 73 subpages (Inner 20 + Outer 50 chapters)
- **Why important:** The most important text of religious Daoism, covering alchemy, immortality practices, talismans, and pharmacology (Inner Chapters), plus Confucian ethics and social criticism (Outer Chapters). Essential for understanding Chinese alchemy and the Daoist tradition.
- **English translation status:** James Ware translated the Inner Chapters (1966, out of print). No complete translation of Outer Chapters.
- **Notes:** Large text but very well structured. Inner Chapters alone would be a strong project.

#### 10. 明儒學案 (Mingru Xue'an)
- **English:** Records of Ming Scholars
- **Author/Era:** Huang Zongxi (黃宗羲), Qing (1676)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/明儒學案
- **Chapters/Length:** 78 subpages (62 juan)
- **Why important:** The first systematic history of philosophy in any language. Huang Zongxi traces the development of Confucian thought through the entire Ming dynasty, school by school. A model for intellectual history worldwide.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Julia Ching translated selections.
- **Notes:** Large but could be done in stages. Pairs with Mingyi Daifang Lu above.

### Literature

#### 11. 儒林外史 (Rulin Waishi)
- **English:** The Scholars / An Unofficial History of the Literati
- **Author/Era:** Wu Jingzi (吳敬梓), Qing (c. 1750)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/儒林外史
- **Chapters/Length:** 56 subpages (56 chapters)
- **Why important:** The greatest satirical novel in Chinese literature. A devastating portrait of the examination system and scholar-official culture, told through loosely connected episodes. Influenced all subsequent Chinese fiction.
- **English translation status:** Yang Hsien-yi and Gladys Yang translated it (1957, Foreign Languages Press) but the translation is not open-access or widely available. No public domain version.
- **Notes:** Perfect size for Deltoi pipeline.

#### 12. 鏡花緣 (Jinghua Yuan)
- **English:** Flowers in the Mirror
- **Author/Era:** Li Ruzhen (李汝珍), Qing (1827)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/鏡花緣
- **Chapters/Length:** 100 subpages (100 chapters)
- **Why important:** A fantastical novel combining satire, proto-feminism, and encyclopedic learning. Features a journey to imaginary countries (Country of Women, Country of Giants, etc.) and argues for women's education and equality. One of the most inventive Chinese novels.
- **English translation status:** Lin Tai-yi translated it (1965, UC Press) but it is heavily abridged and out of print. No complete translation.
- **Notes:** 100 chapters, ideal size.

#### 13. 老殘遊記 (Laocan Youji)
- **English:** The Travels of Lao Can
- **Author/Era:** Liu E (劉鶚), Qing (1903-1906)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/老殘遊記
- **Chapters/Length:** 21 subpages (20 chapters + sequel)
- **Why important:** One of the "Four Novels of Late Qing Denunciation." A lyrical, melancholy novel about a wandering doctor who witnesses injustice and corruption. Famous for its landscape descriptions and its critique of zealous officials who cause more harm than corrupt ones.
- **English translation status:** Harold Shadick translated it (1952, Cornell). Yang Hsien-yi and Gladys Yang also translated it. Both are out of print. No open-access version.
- **Notes:** Short and very manageable.

#### 14. 官場現形記 (Guanchang Xianxing Ji)
- **English:** Officialdom Unmasked / Bureaucracy Exposed
- **Author/Era:** Li Baojia (李寶嘉), Qing (1903-1905)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/官場現形記
- **Chapters/Length:** 60 subpages (60 chapters)
- **Why important:** Another of the "Four Novels of Late Qing Denunciation." The most comprehensive fictional satire of Qing dynasty officialdom: bribery, examination fraud, foreign relations disasters, all portrayed with sharp wit.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation exists. Brief selections only.
- **Notes:** 60 chapters, good size.

#### 15. 花月痕 (Huayue Hen)
- **English:** Traces of Flowers and the Moon
- **Author/Era:** Wei Xiuren (魏秀仁), Qing (c. 1858)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/花月痕
- **Chapters/Length:** 52 subpages (52 chapters)
- **Why important:** A major "scholar and beauty" (才子佳人) novel of the late Qing, interweaving romance with the Taiping Rebellion. Known for its lyrical poetry and its depiction of courtesans and failed scholars.
- **English translation status:** No English translation.
- **Notes:** 52 chapters, manageable size. Entirely untranslated.

#### 16. 海上花列傳 (Haishanghua Liezhuan)
- **English:** The Sing-song Girls of Shanghai
- **Author/Era:** Han Bangqing (韓邦慶), Qing (1892)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/海上花列傳
- **Chapters/Length:** 66 subpages (64 chapters)
- **Why important:** A groundbreaking realist novel set in Shanghai's courtesan houses, written partly in Wu dialect. Praised by scholars as one of the finest late Qing novels. Zhang Ailing (Eileen Chang) championed it and translated it into Mandarin.
- **English translation status:** Eileen Chang translated it into English (published posthumously, 2005, Columbia UP) but it is not open-access.
- **Notes:** 64 chapters. The Wu dialect passages add complexity but the text is mostly in standard vernacular.

#### 17. 隋唐演義 (Sui Tang Yanyi)
- **English:** Romance of the Sui and Tang Dynasties
- **Author/Era:** Chu Renhuo (褚人獲), Qing (c. 1695)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/隋唐演義
- **Chapters/Length:** 102 subpages (100 chapters)
- **Why important:** The great historical novel of the Sui-Tang transition, covering the fall of the Sui dynasty through the rise of the Tang. Features famous characters like Li Shimin, Qin Shubao, and Cheng Yaojin. Source of countless operas and folk tales.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation.
- **Notes:** 100 chapters, same scale as existing Deltoi Chinese novels.

#### 18. 封神演義 (Fengshen Yanyi)
- **English:** Investiture of the Gods
- **Author/Era:** Attributed to Xu Zhonglin (許仲琳), Ming (c. 1620)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/封神演義
- **Chapters/Length:** 103 subpages (100 chapters)
- **Why important:** One of the most popular Chinese mythological novels, telling the story of the overthrow of the Shang dynasty by the Zhou, interwoven with battles between gods, immortals, and demons. Source of many characters in Chinese popular religion.
- **English translation status:** Gu Zhizhong translated it (1992) but the translation is out of print and not open-access. No public domain version.
- **Notes:** 100 chapters. Extremely well-known in Chinese culture.

#### 19. 初刻拍案驚奇 (Chuke Pai'an Jingqi)
- **English:** Slapping the Table in Amazement, First Collection
- **Author/Era:** Ling Mengchu (凌濛初), Ming (1628)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/初刻拍案驚奇
- **Chapters/Length:** 40 subpages (40 stories)
- **Why important:** One of the two great Ming vernacular story collections (the "Erpai"). Alongside Feng Menglong's "Three Words," these constitute the golden age of Chinese short fiction. Tales of love, crime, supernatural, and justice.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. A few individual stories translated in anthologies.
- **Notes:** 40 self-contained stories, ideal for incremental translation.

#### 20. 閱微草堂筆記 (Yuewei Caotang Biji)
- **English:** Random Jottings at the Cottage of Close Scrutiny
- **Author/Era:** Ji Yun (紀昀), Qing (1789-1798)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/閱微草堂筆記
- **Chapters/Length:** 26 subpages (24 juan, ~1200 stories)
- **Why important:** The greatest collection of supernatural tales by China's most powerful literary official (chief editor of the Siku Quanshu). Ji Yun deliberately wrote in a classical, sparse style as a counterpoint to Pu Songling's ornate Liaozhai. Full of wit, moral commentary, and ghost stories.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Some selections in anthologies.
- **Notes:** 24 juan is manageable. Each juan contains many short tales.

#### 21. 浮生六記 (Fusheng Liuji)
- **English:** Six Records of a Floating Life
- **Author/Era:** Shen Fu (沈復), Qing (c. 1808)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/浮生六記
- **Chapters/Length:** 6 subpages (6 chapters, 4 extant)
- **Why important:** One of the most beloved Chinese autobiographical works. A tender memoir of married life, travel, gardening, and aesthetic pleasures, shadowed by poverty and loss. Universally admired for its emotional honesty and lyrical prose.
- **English translation status:** Leonard Pratt and Chiang Su-hui translated it (Penguin, 1983). Lin Yutang also translated selections. Not open-access.
- **Notes:** Very short (4 extant chapters). Could be translated quickly.

#### 22. 陶庵夢憶 (Tao'an Mengyi)
- **English:** Dream Memories of Tao'an
- **Author/Era:** Zhang Dai (張岱), Ming-Qing transition (c. 1665)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/陶庵夢憶
- **Chapters/Length:** 12 subpages (8 juan, ~127 essays)
- **Why important:** The finest collection of nostalgic essays in Chinese literature. Zhang Dai, a ruined Ming aristocrat, recalls the vanished world of late Ming Jiangnan: opera performances, tea ceremonies, lantern festivals, lake excursions. Each essay is a miniature masterpiece of sensory recall.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Philip Kafalas translated selections.
- **Notes:** Short essays, very high literary quality.

#### 23. 閑情偶寄 (Xianqing Ouji)
- **English:** Random Thoughts on Idle Pleasures
- **Author/Era:** Li Yu (李漁), Qing (1671)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/閑情偶寄
- **Chapters/Length:** 45 subpages (covering drama, music, costume, gardening, food, architecture, health)
- **Why important:** The most comprehensive manual of aesthetics and daily life in Chinese literature. Li Yu covers everything from how to write plays to how to arrange a garden, from cooking to window design. Witty, opinionated, and endlessly entertaining.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Patrick Hanan translated selections.
- **Notes:** 45 subpages, well-structured by topic.

#### 24. 笑林廣記 (Xiaolin Guangji)
- **English:** Expanded Records of the Forest of Laughter
- **Author/Era:** "Youzhi Daoren" (游戲道人), Qing (1787)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/笑林廣記
- **Chapters/Length:** 13 subpages (12 categories, hundreds of jokes)
- **Why important:** The largest and most famous Chinese joke collection. Jokes organized by category (stupid people, ridiculous officials, stingy men, marital humor, etc.). A window into Qing popular culture and humor.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Very few jokes translated.
- **Notes:** Short entries, fun to translate. Some ribald humor.

#### 25. 聊齋志異 (Liaozhai Zhiyi)
- **English:** Strange Tales from a Chinese Studio
- **Author/Era:** Pu Songling (蒲松齡), Qing (c. 1679-1740)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/聊齋志異
- **Chapters/Length:** 14 subpages (12 juan, ~500 stories)
- **Why important:** The most celebrated collection of supernatural fiction in Chinese literature. Fox spirits, ghosts, scholars, and examinations intertwine in stories that range from romantic to horrific to satirical. A towering achievement of classical Chinese prose.
- **English translation status:** Herbert Giles (1880) and John Minford (2006, Penguin) translated substantial selections, but no complete translation exists. Only ~100 of 500+ stories translated.
- **Notes:** 12 juan with many stories. The untranslated 400+ stories alone are a major gap.

### Literary Criticism

#### 26. 文心雕龍 (Wenxin Diaolong)
- **English:** The Literary Mind and the Carving of Dragons
- **Author/Era:** Liu Xie (劉勰), Southern Liang (c. 501-502 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/文心雕龍
- **Chapters/Length:** 51 subpages (50 chapters in 10 juan)
- **Why important:** The most systematic and comprehensive work of literary theory in Chinese history. Covers every aspect of literary creation: genre, style, rhetoric, imagination, structure, tradition, and evaluation. Foundational for East Asian literary studies.
- **English translation status:** Vincent Shih translated it (1959, reprinted 2015). Yang Guobin also translated it (2003). Neither is open-access.
- **Notes:** 50 chapters, manageable size. Parallel prose (駢文) style is challenging but rewarding.

#### 27. 滄浪詩話 (Canglang Shihua)
- **English:** Canglang's Remarks on Poetry
- **Author/Era:** Yan Yu (嚴羽), Song (c. 1230)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/滄浪詩話
- **Chapters/Length:** 6 subpages (5 sections)
- **Why important:** The most influential work of poetry criticism in Chinese history. Yan Yu's theory of poetry as "inspired awakening" (妙悟) rather than learned craft shaped poetry criticism for 700 years. His ranking of poets by dynasties became canonical.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Selections in anthologies.
- **Notes:** Very short text. Could be translated quickly.

#### 28. 隨園詩話 (Suiyuan Shihua)
- **English:** Random Notes on Poetry from the Sui Garden
- **Author/Era:** Yuan Mei (袁枚), Qing (1790)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/隨園詩話
- **Chapters/Length:** 16 subpages (16 juan + supplement)
- **Why important:** The most entertaining and wide-ranging collection of poetry criticism in Chinese. Yuan Mei combines anecdotes, critical judgments, and hundreds of quoted poems. Champions "spirit and feeling" (性靈) over formalism.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. J.J. Schmidt translated selections.
- **Notes:** 16 juan, readable and anecdotal.

#### 29. 人間詞話 (Renjian Cihua)
- **English:** Remarks on Ci Poetry in the Human World
- **Author/Era:** Wang Guowei (王國維), Qing-Republic (1908-1910)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/人間詞話
- **Chapters/Length:** Single page (64 remarks in published version, 127 in complete)
- **Why important:** The most influential work of modern Chinese literary criticism. Wang Guowei's concept of jingjie (境界, "realm" or "world") became the dominant framework for Chinese aesthetic judgment. Bridges traditional Chinese poetics with Western aesthetics.
- **English translation status:** Adele Austin Rickett translated it (1977). Tu Ching-i also published a version. Neither widely available.
- **Notes:** Very short. Could be done in a single session.

#### 30. 詩品 (Shipin)
- **English:** Grades of Poetry
- **Author/Era:** Zhong Rong (鍾嶸), Southern Liang (c. 513-518)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/詩品
- **Chapters/Length:** 3 subpages (3 grades: upper, middle, lower)
- **Why important:** The earliest systematic ranking and critical evaluation of Chinese poets. Zhong Rong grades 122 poets from the Han to his own day into three tiers, with critical comments on each. Established the practice of comparative criticism.
- **English translation status:** No complete standalone English translation. J.Y. Liu discussed it at length.
- **Notes:** Very short. Ideal quick translation.

### Science, Medicine, and Technology

#### 31. 天工開物 (Tiangong Kaiwu)
- **English:** The Exploitation of the Works of Nature
- **Author/Era:** Song Yingxing (宋應星), Ming (1637)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/天工開物
- **Chapters/Length:** 158 subpages (18 chapters covering agriculture, textiles, ceramics, metallurgy, etc.)
- **Why important:** The most comprehensive encyclopedia of technology in pre-modern China. Covers every major industry: grain cultivation, silk weaving, salt production, sugar refining, ceramics, bronze and iron casting, shipbuilding, papermaking, and more. Lavishly illustrated.
- **English translation status:** E-tu Zen Sun and Shiou-chuan Sun translated it (1966, Penn State). Out of print and not open-access.
- **Notes:** 18 chapters with many subsections (158 pages). Very well structured.

#### 32. 夢溪筆談 (Mengxi Bitan)
- **English:** Dream Pool Essays
- **Author/Era:** Shen Kuo (沈括), Song (1088)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/夢溪筆談
- **Chapters/Length:** 29 subpages (26 juan + supplements)
- **Why important:** The most important work of scientific observation in pre-modern China. Shen Kuo records discoveries and observations across astronomy, mathematics, geology, medicine, music, archaeology, and technology. Describes movable type printing, the magnetic compass needle, and fossil formation.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Selected passages translated in various science history anthologies.
- **Notes:** 26 juan of short entries. Biji (notebook) format makes it very suitable for incremental translation.

#### 33. 齊民要術 (Qimin Yaoshu)
- **English:** Essential Techniques for the Common People
- **Author/Era:** Jia Sixie (賈思勰), Northern Wei (c. 544 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/齊民要術
- **Chapters/Length:** 14 subpages (10 juan, 92 chapters)
- **Why important:** The oldest and most comprehensive agricultural manual surviving from China. Covers crop cultivation, animal husbandry, food processing, brewing, and fermentation. An invaluable source for economic and environmental history.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Selections in science history anthologies.
- **Notes:** Technical vocabulary requires careful handling.

#### 34. 黃帝內經 (Huangdi Neijing)
- **English:** The Yellow Emperor's Classic of Internal Medicine
- **Author/Era:** Attributed to the Yellow Emperor, compiled Han dynasty
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/黃帝內經
- **Chapters/Length:** 36 subpages (Suwen 81 + Lingshu 81 chapters)
- **Why important:** The foundational text of Chinese medicine. Establishes the theoretical framework of yin-yang, five phases, meridians, and qi that still underlies traditional Chinese medicine today. A dialogue between the Yellow Emperor and his physician Qi Bo.
- **English translation status:** Ilza Veith translated Suwen chapters 1-34 (1949/1966). Paul Unschuld published a complete annotated Suwen translation (2011, UC Press) but it is expensive and academic-only. No open-access complete translation.
- **Notes:** 162 chapters total. Could focus on Suwen (81 chapters) first.

#### 35. 本草綱目 (Bencao Gangmu)
- **English:** Compendium of Materia Medica
- **Author/Era:** Li Shizhen (李時珍), Ming (1578, published 1596)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/本草綱目
- **Chapters/Length:** 66 subpages (52 juan, ~1,900 entries)
- **Why important:** The greatest pharmacological work in Chinese history and one of the most comprehensive natural history texts in the pre-modern world. Catalogues 1,892 drugs derived from plants, animals, and minerals. UNESCO Memory of the World.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Luo Xiwen published partial translations. Individual entries translated in various studies.
- **Notes:** Very large (52 juan) but could be done selectively by category. Each entry is relatively self-contained.

### Geography and Travel

#### 36. 水經注 (Shuijing Zhu)
- **English:** Commentary on the Waterways Classic
- **Author/Era:** Li Daoyuan (酈道元), Northern Wei (c. 515-524 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/水經注
- **Chapters/Length:** 41 subpages (40 juan)
- **Why important:** The greatest geographical text of pre-modern China. Li Daoyuan expanded a terse catalog of rivers into a richly detailed account of landscapes, cities, legends, and inscriptions along China's waterways. As much a literary masterpiece as a geographical work.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Selections in geography anthologies.
- **Notes:** 40 juan, beautiful prose. A major gap in English-language sinology.

#### 37. 徐霞客遊記 (Xu Xiake Youji)
- **English:** The Travel Diaries of Xu Xiake
- **Author/Era:** Xu Xiake (徐霞客), Ming (1613-1639, published posthumously)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/徐霞客遊記
- **Chapters/Length:** 57 subpages
- **Why important:** The travel journals of China's greatest explorer-geographer. Xu Xiake spent 30 years traveling across China, recording landscapes, caves, rivers, and geology with scientific precision and literary flair. His karst geology observations predate Western science by centuries.
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Li Chi translated the Yunnan section (1974). Selections in travel anthologies.
- **Notes:** Large text but very readable. Could be translated by region.

#### 38. 大唐西域記 (Datang Xiyu Ji)
- **English:** Records of the Western Regions of the Great Tang
- **Author/Era:** Xuanzang (玄奘), dictated to Bianji (辯機), Tang (646 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/大唐西域記
- **Chapters/Length:** 14 subpages (12 juan)
- **Why important:** Xuanzang's firsthand account of his 17-year journey to India (629-645 CE). Describes 138 kingdoms across Central and South Asia with detailed observations on geography, politics, religion, and customs. An irreplaceable source for the history of Buddhism and Central Asian civilization.
- **English translation status:** Samuel Beal translated it (1884, public domain but very dated). Li Rongxi published a modern translation (1996, BDK) in limited circulation. No widely available modern version.
- **Notes:** 12 juan, manageable size. Beal's 1884 translation is freely available but desperately needs updating.

#### 39. 洛陽伽藍記 (Luoyang Qielan Ji)
- **English:** A Record of Buddhist Monasteries in Luoyang
- **Author/Era:** Yang Xuanzhi (楊衒之), Northern Wei-Eastern Wei (c. 547 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/洛陽伽藍記
- **Chapters/Length:** 6 subpages (5 juan)
- **Why important:** A unique blend of urban geography, architectural description, political history, and nostalgic elegy. Yang Xuanzhi describes the Buddhist monasteries of Luoyang, the Northern Wei capital, after its destruction. Through the monasteries he tells the story of the dynasty's rise and fall.
- **English translation status:** Yi-t'ung Wang translated it (1984, Princeton). Not open-access.
- **Notes:** Very short (5 juan). Compact and beautiful.

### History and Biji (Notebook) Literature

#### 40. 世說新語 (Shishuo Xinyu)
- **English:** A New Account of Tales of the World
- **Author/Era:** Liu Yiqing (劉義慶), Southern Song (c. 430 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/世說新語
- **Chapters/Length:** 38 subpages (36 categories, ~1,130 anecdotes)
- **Why important:** The most influential collection of character sketches and anecdotes in Chinese literature. Captures the wit, eccentricity, and aesthetic sensibility of the Wei-Jin aristocracy in brief, brilliant vignettes. Established the "pure conversation" (清談) tradition.
- **English translation status:** Richard Mather translated it (1976, reprinted 2002, Michigan). Not open-access but well-regarded. No public domain version.
- **Notes:** 36 categories of short anecdotes. Very well suited to translation.

#### 41. 搜神記 (Soushen Ji)
- **English:** In Search of the Supernatural
- **Author/Era:** Gan Bao (干寶), Eastern Jin (c. 350 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/搜神記
- **Chapters/Length:** 21 subpages (20 juan)
- **Why important:** The earliest major collection of supernatural tales in Chinese literature. Ghosts, spirits, omens, transformations, and divine interventions drawn from earlier sources and contemporary accounts. Source for countless later stories and operas.
- **English translation status:** Kenneth DeWoskin and J.I. Crump translated it (1996). Not open-access.
- **Notes:** 20 juan of short tales. Very readable.

#### 42. 容齋隨筆 (Rongzhai Suibi)
- **English:** Random Notes from the Rongzhai Studio
- **Author/Era:** Hong Mai (洪邁), Song (1163-1202)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/容齋隨筆
- **Chapters/Length:** 18 subpages (5 collections, 74 juan total)
- **Why important:** The most scholarly and wide-ranging biji collection of the Song dynasty. Hong Mai covers history, literature, philology, politics, and daily life with extraordinary erudition. Emperor Taizu of Ming read a chapter every day.
- **English translation status:** No English translation at all.
- **Notes:** Large text (74 juan across 5 collections). Could translate the first collection (16 juan) as a start.

#### 43. 困學紀聞 (Kunxue Jiwen)
- **English:** Records of Learning Difficulties
- **Author/Era:** Wang Yinglin (王應麟), Song (c. 1270s)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/困學紀聞
- **Chapters/Length:** 20 subpages (20 juan)
- **Why important:** One of the greatest works of evidential scholarship in Chinese history. Wang Yinglin meticulously investigates textual problems across the classics, histories, and philosophy. A precursor to the Qing evidential research movement.
- **English translation status:** No English translation.
- **Notes:** 20 juan, scholarly but fascinating.

#### 44. 酉陽雜俎 (Youyang Zazu)
- **English:** Miscellaneous Morsels from Youyang
- **Author/Era:** Duan Chengshi (段成式), Tang (c. 860 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/酉陽雜俎
- **Chapters/Length:** 31 subpages (20 juan + 10 juan supplement)
- **Why important:** The most fascinating and eclectic biji collection of the Tang dynasty. Covers natural history, folklore, foreign lands, Buddhist and Daoist lore, food, drugs, ghosts, and oddities. Contains the earliest known Chinese version of the Cinderella story ("Ye Xian").
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Individual entries translated in various studies.
- **Notes:** 30 juan, short entries. Endlessly entertaining.

#### 45. 老學庵筆記 (Laoxue'an Biji)
- **English:** Notes from the Old Scholar's Studio
- **Author/Era:** Lu You (陸游), Song (c. 1190s)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/老學庵筆記
- **Chapters/Length:** 10 subpages (10 juan)
- **Why important:** Personal notes by one of the greatest Song poets. Lu You records anecdotes about politics, literature, customs, and daily life in the Southern Song. Vivid and personal, full of eyewitness accounts of the Jurchen wars.
- **English translation status:** No English translation.
- **Notes:** Short (10 juan). Very readable.

#### 46. 東京夢華錄 (Dongjing Menghua Lu)
- **English:** Dreams of Splendor of the Eastern Capital
- **Author/Era:** Meng Yuanlao (孟元老), Song (1147)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/東京夢華錄
- **Chapters/Length:** 10 subpages (10 juan)
- **Why important:** The most detailed account of urban life in the Northern Song capital, Kaifeng, before its fall to the Jurchen in 1127. Markets, restaurants, festivals, entertainment, architecture, and daily customs described with nostalgic precision. Essential for Song social history.
- **English translation status:** Stephen West translated it (2010, "Records of the Splendors of the Eastern Capital"). Published in limited academic edition, not widely available.
- **Notes:** Short (10 juan). Pairs with Meng Liang Lu and Wulin Jiushi for a complete picture of Song urban life.

#### 47. 池北偶談 (Chibei Outan)
- **English:** Random Talks North of the Pond
- **Author/Era:** Wang Shizhen (王士禛), Qing (c. 1691)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/池北偶談
- **Chapters/Length:** 27 subpages (26 juan)
- **Why important:** A major biji collection by the leading poet-critic of the early Qing. Covers literature, history, supernatural tales, and contemporary events. Wang Shizhen was the arbiter of taste for his generation.
- **English translation status:** No English translation.
- **Notes:** 26 juan, wide-ranging.

#### 48. 山海經 (Shanhai Jing)
- **English:** Classic of Mountains and Seas
- **Author/Era:** Anonymous, compiled Warring States-Han (4th c. BCE - 1st c. CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/山海經
- **Chapters/Length:** 19 subpages (18 chapters)
- **Why important:** The most important mythological and geographical text of ancient China. Describes mountains, seas, peoples, animals, plants, minerals, and spirits across the known world. Source of countless myths (Nüwa, Gun and Yu, Kuafu chasing the sun). Foundational for Chinese mythology studies.
- **English translation status:** Anne Birrell translated it (1999, Penguin). Richard Strassberg also published an illustrated version (2002, UC Press). Neither is open-access.
- **Notes:** 18 chapters, compact. The text is very terse and benefits from annotation.

#### 49. 貞觀政要 (Zhenguan Zhengyao)
- **English:** Essentials of Government from the Zhenguan Era
- **Author/Era:** Wu Jing (吳兢), Tang (c. 708-710 CE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/貞觀政要
- **Chapters/Length:** 10 subpages (10 juan, 40 chapters)
- **Why important:** A record of the model governance of Emperor Taizong of Tang (r. 626-649), compiled from court discussions. Covers personnel selection, remonstrance, frugality, justice, and the ruler-minister relationship. Became a mirror-for-princes text across East Asia (translated into Khitan, Jurchen, Mongol, Manchu, Japanese, and Korean).
- **English translation status:** No complete English translation. Selections in various anthologies.
- **Notes:** 40 chapters in 10 juan. Dialogue format.

#### 50. 春秋繁露 (Chunqiu Fanlu)
- **English:** Luxuriant Dew of the Spring and Autumn Annals
- **Author/Era:** Dong Zhongshu (董仲舒), Western Han (c. 150-100 BCE)
- **Wikisource URL:** https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/春秋繁露
- **Chapters/Length:** 17 subpages (17 juan, 82 chapters)
- **Why important:** The foundational text of Han Confucian cosmology. Dong Zhongshu synthesized Confucian ethics with yin-yang and five-phases cosmology, establishing the theoretical basis for the imperial state ideology that lasted two millennia. His theory of Heaven-human correlation (天人感應) shaped Chinese political thought profoundly.
- **English translation status:** Sarah Queen and John Major published a complete translation (2016, Columbia UP) but it is not open-access. No public domain translation.
- **Notes:** 82 chapters in 17 juan. Moderate size.
